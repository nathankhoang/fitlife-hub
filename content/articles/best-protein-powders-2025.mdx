**Key Takeaways**

- Aim for **0.7–1.0 g of protein per pound of bodyweight** daily — a 160 lb person needs 112–160 g
- Whey absorbs in **1–2 hours**; casein takes **5–7 hours**; plant blends fall in the **2–3 hour** range
- Whey concentrate typically costs **$0.03–0.05 per gram of protein**; isolate runs **$0.05–0.08**; quality plant blends often reach **$0.07–0.10**
- To match whey's leucine content per serving, plant protein users need **roughly 10–15% more total protein** — about 25–28 g versus whey's 24 g
- The post-workout anabolic window is **real but wide** — muscle protein synthesis stays elevated for up to 2 hours after training
- A single shake maxes out muscle protein synthesis at **~20–40 g**; anything beyond that is oxidized, not muscle

## What to Look For in a Protein Powder

Before buying, consider:

- **Protein per serving** — aim for 20–30 g
- **Leucine content** — drives muscle protein synthesis; whey naturally delivers ~2.5 g leucine per 25 g serving
- **Ingredients** — avoid excessive fillers and artificial sweeteners
- **Taste and mixability** — you have to actually drink it
- **Price per serving** — calculate cost per gram of protein, not per scoop

### Whey Concentrate

The most common and affordable form. Contains some lactose and fat. Great for post-workout nutrition at a price that lets you stay consistent without budget strain.

### Whey Isolate

Further filtered to remove most lactose and fat. Higher protein percentage per serving. Better for lactose-sensitive individuals or those tracking macros tightly.

### Casein

Digests slowly — ideal for a nighttime protein source to fuel overnight muscle repair. Also useful as a between-meal option to suppress appetite and sustain amino acid delivery.

### Plant-Based

Pea, rice, and hemp blends for those avoiding dairy. A **single-source** plant protein (pea alone, rice alone) is incomplete — it will lack adequate levels of certain essential amino acids. Always choose a **blended** plant protein for a full amino acid profile.

## The LBE Protein Powder Selection Framework

Most buyers overthink protein powder. This three-tier framework cuts through the noise by matching your actual situation to the right product type.

**Tier 1 — Leucine-First (Muscle Building Priority)**

If your main goal is hypertrophy, choose a whey concentrate or isolate with at least 2.3 g of leucine per serving. Leucine is the primary trigger for muscle protein synthesis — it's the single most important amino acid metric on the label. Optimum Nutrition Gold Standard and MyProtein Impact Whey both meet this bar.

**Tier 2 — Budget Ceiling (Cost-Per-Gram Discipline)**

Calculate your cost per gram of protein, not per scoop. Divide the price of the container by total grams of protein inside. A good target: under $0.05/g for concentrate, under $0.08/g for isolate. Many premium-branded powders charge $0.10–0.15/g for no meaningful quality advantage.

**Tier 3 — Elimination Filters (Dietary & Lifestyle Constraints)**

Apply these last, after Tiers 1 and 2:
- **Lactose intolerant** → isolate or plant blend
- **Vegan / dairy-free** → pea + rice blend (not single-source)
- **Convenience over shakes** → protein bars as a supplement to, not replacement for, powder
- **Nighttime recovery focus** → casein, 30–40 g before bed

Work through all three tiers in order. Most people jump straight to Tier 3 and end up paying too much for a product that underdelivers on what actually matters.

## How Much Protein Do You Actually Need?

Research suggests **0.7–1 g of protein per pound of bodyweight** is optimal for muscle building. For a 160-pound person, that's 112–160 g of protein per day.

Protein powder should supplement your diet, not replace whole food sources. Prioritize chicken, eggs, fish, beef, and legumes first — these come with micronutrients and satiety that powder cannot replicate.

## When to Take Protein Powder

- **Post-workout** — within 1–2 hours after training while muscle protein synthesis is elevated
- **Morning** — if your breakfast is low in protein (under 20 g), add a shake
- **Between meals** — to close the gap toward your daily target without a full meal
- **Before bed** — casein specifically, to sustain amino acid delivery through the overnight fast

## The Contrarian Take: Plant Protein Is Not Inferior — But You Need More of It

The fitness world has long treated whey as the unambiguous king and plant protein as a consolation prize. The research tells a more nuanced story.

A 2019 study comparing whey and pea protein in resistance-trained men found **no significant difference in muscle thickness gains** after 12 weeks when total protein intake was equated. The key phrase: *when total protein was equated*.

The actual limitation of plant protein is **leucine density**, not quality per se. Whey concentrate delivers approximately **2.5 g of leucine per 25 g serving**. A comparable pea protein serving delivers roughly **1.8–2.0 g of leucine** — below the ~2.5 g threshold believed to maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis.

The fix is straightforward: **increase the serving size by 10–15%**. Instead of 25 g of plant protein, take 28–30 g. This closes the leucine gap without requiring a different product.

Bottom line: if you're vegan or dairy-free, a quality pea + rice blend at a slightly larger serving size is just as effective as whey for muscle building. The inferior-protein narrative is outdated — it was based on studies that didn't equate leucine or total protein intake.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Relying only on powder** — real food protein sources are more satiating and nutrient-dense; powder is a supplement, not a staple
2. **Taking too much per sitting** — more than 40 g in one dose provides diminishing returns for muscle protein synthesis; spread intake across 3–4 meals
3. **Ignoring total daily intake** — timing matters far less than consistently hitting your daily protein target
4. **Choosing by brand over label math** — always check protein per serving, leucine content, and cost per gram before buying
