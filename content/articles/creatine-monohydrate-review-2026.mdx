## How Creatine Actually Works

Your muscles use a molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate) as their primary fuel for short, explosive efforts — think the first 10 seconds of a heavy set or sprint. When ATP is burned, it becomes ADP, and your body has to regenerate it to keep going. Creatine's job, stored as phosphocreatine in muscle tissue, is to donate a phosphate group and speed that regeneration.

More phosphocreatine in the tank means more reps before failure, more power per rep, and faster recovery between sets. The catch: most people only hit roughly 60–80% of their natural phosphocreatine storage capacity from diet alone (primarily red meat and fish). Supplementing with creatine monohydrate saturates those stores to 100%, which is where the performance gains come from.

A 2021 meta-analysis in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition* pooled data from **over 200 controlled trials** and found that creatine supplementation combined with resistance training produced, on average, a **~15% increase in training volume** (total weight moved across a session) compared with placebo. That's not a marketing number — that's decades of independent research converging on the same finding.

## Dosing: How Much and When

This is where most beginners overcomplicate things. The evidence-based protocol is genuinely boring:

- **Daily dose:** 3–5 grams per day, every day, forever (or for as long as you're training).
- **Loading phase:** Unnecessary. The old "20g per day for 5–7 days" loading protocol saturates your muscles about 2 weeks faster, but a standard 3–5g daily dose gets you to the same place by week 3–4 without the GI distress.
- **Timing:** Doesn't meaningfully matter. Morning, pre-workout, post-workout, with food, without food — pick whatever you'll actually remember. Consistency beats timing.
- **Cycling:** Not needed. Your body doesn't downregulate creatine production in any clinically meaningful way from chronic supplementation.

A small nuance: taking creatine with carbs or a protein-containing meal can slightly improve uptake via insulin response, but the effect is marginal. If you eat in the next couple of hours, you've covered it.

## Monohydrate vs HCl, Kre-Alkalyn, HMB, and the Rest

Here's where we debunk the shelf full of expensive alternatives marketed as "next generation" creatine.

**Creatine HCl** is creatine bonded with hydrochloric acid. The pitch is "better solubility, smaller dose, no bloating." The reality: when researchers compare equivalent doses of HCl and monohydrate head-to-head for strength and lean mass outcomes, they consistently find no meaningful difference. You're paying 3–5x more per serving for marketing copy.

**Kre-Alkalyn** (buffered creatine) claims its higher pH prevents breakdown into creatinine in the stomach. A 2012 randomized trial published in the *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition* directly compared kre-alkalyn to monohydrate over 28 days. Result: monohydrate produced equal or greater gains in strength, lean mass, and muscle creatine content — at a fraction of the cost.

**HMB** (beta-hydroxy beta-methylbutyrate) is sometimes marketed alongside creatine, but it's a different compound entirely. The evidence for HMB in trained lifters is mixed at best and its effect size is far smaller than creatine's.

**Liquid creatine and creatine ethyl ester** are actively worse — both degrade into creatinine (a useless byproduct) more quickly than monohydrate.

The bottom line: every "upgraded" form of creatine is trying to solve problems that monohydrate doesn't really have. Micronized monohydrate mixes cleanly in water, absorbs almost completely, and has three decades of safety data behind it. Buy the plain white powder.

## Side Effects: What's Real, What's Myth

Let's be honest about trade-offs, because a fair creatine monohydrate review shouldn't pretend there are none.

**Water weight (real, temporary, misunderstood):** Creatine pulls water *into* your muscle cells, not under your skin. Expect 2–4 pounds of scale weight in the first few weeks. This is intramuscular hydration, not bloat — your muscles will actually look slightly fuller. If you're weight-class-sensitive (wrestling, MMA), factor it in. Otherwise, ignore the scale for a month.

**GI discomfort (real, dose-dependent):** Roughly 5–10% of users report mild stomach upset, especially when loading or taking more than 5g at once. Fix: split into two 2–3g doses or take it with food.

**Kidney damage (myth, with one exception):** Decades of research on healthy adults — including long-term studies up to 5 years — have found no adverse effects on kidney function. The one exception: if you have pre-existing kidney disease, talk to a doctor before supplementing anything.

**Hair loss (extremely weak evidence):** One 2009 rugby study showed an increase in DHT, a hormone associated with male-pattern baldness. No follow-up study has replicated the finding, and no study has directly shown hair loss from creatine. If you're already genetically predisposed and paranoid, it's a fair thing to monitor, but the panic is overblown.

## Who It's For (and Who Should Skip)

**Strong match:**
- Beginners lifting 2+ times per week who want to accelerate their first 6 months of gains
- Intermediate and advanced lifters looking for an extra 5–15% on their work sets
- Vegetarians and vegans (who have lower baseline muscle creatine stores)
- Anyone in power sports, sprinting, or combat sports

**Probably skip:**
- Endurance-only athletes (marathon runners, cyclists) — the performance benefit is minimal for long-duration, low-power efforts, and you probably don't want the extra water weight
- Anyone with diagnosed kidney disease
- People who aren't actually training consistently — creatine amplifies the results of hard work; it doesn't replace it

## Pairing Creatine with Protein

Creatine gives your muscles more short-term fuel. Protein gives them the raw material to repair and grow after you've used that fuel. These two work on entirely different mechanisms, which is exactly why stacking them is so effective — and why this is where most beginner supplement routines should start and stop.

A reasonable beginner protocol: 3–5g of creatine monohydrate daily, plus 0.7–1.0 grams of protein per pound of bodyweight spread across 3–4 meals. If your whole-food protein intake is falling short (and for most beginners, it is), a quality whey protein fills the gap without overhauling your diet. Whey is particularly useful post-workout because of its fast absorption and high leucine content, which is the amino acid most directly responsible for triggering muscle protein synthesis.

You don't need to take creatine and whey together, but mixing them in the same post-workout shake is convenient and there's no downside to doing so. Both dissolve well in water or milk, and the combo covers the two most important supplement bases a beginner will ever need.
