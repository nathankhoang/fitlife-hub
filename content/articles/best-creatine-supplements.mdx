## What Is Creatine?

Creatine is a naturally occurring compound found in muscle cells. It helps produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — the primary energy currency for high-intensity exercise. Supplementing creatine increases your muscle phosphocreatine stores, allowing you to train harder before fatiguing.

---

## The Proven Benefits

- **Increased strength** — meta-analyses show 5–10% improvement in 1RM strength
- **More reps** — enables 1–2 additional reps at submaximal loads
- **Greater muscle mass** — through improved training volume and cell volumization
- **Faster recovery** — reduces muscle cell damage and inflammation
- **Cognitive benefits** — emerging evidence for brain health and memory

## How to Take Creatine

### Quick-Reference Dosing Cheat Sheet

| Protocol | Daily Dose | Duration to Saturation | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Maintenance only | 3–5 g | ~3–4 weeks | Easiest; recommended for most |
| Loading phase | 20 g (4 × 5 g) | ~5–7 days | Faster saturation, no long-term advantage |
| Post-loading maintenance | 3–5 g | Already saturated | Continue indefinitely |
| Higher body weight (&gt;100 kg) | 5 g | ~4 weeks | Upper end of standard range |
| Vegetarian/vegan | 5 g | May need less time | Lower baseline = faster apparent response |

### Loading Phase (Optional)

Take 20 g per day (divided into 4 doses of 5 g) for 5–7 days. This saturates muscle stores faster.

### Maintenance Phase

Take 3–5 g per day. This is all you need to maintain elevated creatine levels once saturated.

### When to Take It

Timing doesn't matter much. Take it whenever it's convenient — with a meal is fine. Consistency is what matters.

## Contrarian Take: The Loading Phase Is Oversold

The fitness industry treats the loading phase as gospel. Load for a week, they say — otherwise you're leaving gains on the table. The research tells a different story.

A 2003 study in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* (Hultman et al.) found that while loading reaches muscle saturation faster, **total muscle creatine levels at 28 days are identical** whether you loaded or went straight to 3–5 g/day. You reach the same endpoint — loading just gets you there in 1 week instead of 4.

For the vast majority of lifters, that three-week difference is irrelevant. You're not competing in 10 days. You're building a training habit over months. Starting with 5 g/day and skipping the bloating, GI discomfort, and expense of 20 g/day loading is the smarter default for most people.

**Who should actually load:** Competitive athletes with a specific event in 2–3 weeks who want maximum saturation quickly. Everyone else — skip it, stay consistent, get the same result.

## Common Creatine Questions

**Does it cause water retention?**
Creatine draws water into muscle cells (intracellular), which increases muscle fullness and size. This is a good thing. It does not cause bloating or subcutaneous water retention.

**Is it safe long-term?**
Yes. Studies spanning up to 5 years show no negative effects on kidney or liver function in healthy individuals.

**Do I need to cycle off it?**
No. Cycling creatine is not supported by research. You can take it continuously.

**Will it work if I'm a vegetarian?**
Potentially more so. Vegetarians have lower baseline muscle creatine levels and tend to respond more dramatically to supplementation.

---

## Who Shouldn't Take Creatine?

People with pre-existing kidney disease should consult a physician before supplementing. Everyone else — it's one of the safest supplements available.
