Creatine is the most studied supplement in sports nutrition history. Over 500 peer-reviewed studies confirm its safety and efficacy for improving strength, power output, and lean muscle mass. If you're not taking it, you're leaving performance on the table.

## What Is Creatine?

Creatine is a naturally occurring compound found in muscle cells. It helps produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — the primary energy currency for high-intensity exercise. Supplementing creatine increases your muscle phosphocreatine stores, allowing you to train harder before fatiguing.

## The Proven Benefits

- **Increased strength** — meta-analyses show 5–10% improvement in 1RM strength
- **More reps** — enables 1–2 additional reps at submaximal loads
- **Greater muscle mass** — through improved training volume and cell volumization
- **Faster recovery** — reduces muscle cell damage and inflammation
- **Cognitive benefits** — emerging evidence for brain health and memory

## How to Take Creatine

### Loading Phase (Optional)
Take 20g per day (divided into 4 doses of 5g) for 5–7 days. This saturates muscle stores faster.

### Maintenance Phase
Take 3–5g per day. This is all you need to maintain elevated creatine levels once saturated.

**Loading is not required.** You can skip straight to 5g/day — it just takes 3–4 weeks to reach full saturation instead of 1 week.

### When to Take It
Timing doesn't matter much. Take it whenever it's convenient — with a meal is fine. Consistency is what matters.

## Common Creatine Questions

**Does it cause water retention?**
Creatine draws water _into_ muscle cells (intracellular), which increases muscle fullness and size. This is a good thing. It does not cause bloating or subcutaneous water retention.

**Is it safe long-term?**
Yes. Studies spanning up to 5 years show no negative effects on kidney or liver function in healthy individuals.

**Do I need to cycle off it?**
No. Cycling creatine is not supported by research. You can take it continuously.

**Will it work if I'm a vegetarian?**
Potentially more so. Vegetarians have lower baseline muscle creatine levels and tend to respond more dramatically to supplementation.

## Who Shouldn't Take Creatine?

People with pre-existing kidney disease should consult a physician before supplementing. Everyone else — it's one of the safest supplements available.
